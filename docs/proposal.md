# Problem Description
Medical vision–language models (VLMs) have recently shown promise in generating natural language explanations for clinical images by integrating structured knowledge graphs with deep visual representations. Systems such as KG-LLaVA retrieve radiological findings from single-domain knowledge graphs constructed from imaging reports to support explainable medical image understanding. While these models produce technically accurate descriptions of visual abnormalities, they remain limited to surface-level radiological observations and do not reflect the broader clinical reasoning process used by physicians.
In real-world clinical decision making, interpretation of medical images extends beyond identifying visual findings. Clinicians reason about underlying disease mechanisms, connect imaging patterns to patient symptoms, and consider treatment implications when forming diagnoses. For example, identifying pulmonary consolidation is clinically meaningful only when linked to disease progression, physiological impact, and appropriate therapeutic interventions. Current single-domain knowledge graph retrieval approaches fail to capture this multi-layered clinical context.
This limitation results in explanations that are structurally correct but clinically incomplete, restricting their usefulness in real medical settings where holistic understanding is critical. The absence of disease pathophysiology and pharmacological knowledge prevents VLMs from generating explanations that mirror expert clinical reasoning or support downstream medical decision processes.
To address this gap, we propose a multi-domain knowledge graph retrieval framework that extends KG-LLaVA by integrating radiology knowledge with disease pathophysiology extracted from clinical narratives and pharmacological knowledge from electronic health records and curated drug databases. By selectively retrieving relevant information across domains, the model aims to generate explanations that combine visual observations with clinical mechanisms and treatment context, producing richer, more comprehensive, and clinically meaningful medical image explanations.


# Base Paper
Li et al. (2024) – KG-LLaVA: Knowledge Graph-Augmented Vision–Language Model for Medical Explanations
In the work by Li et al. [2024], the authors introduced KG-LLaVA, a knowledge graph retrieval-augmented vision–language model designed to generate explainable natural language descriptions for thoracic medical images. They constructed a radiology-focused knowledge graph using RadGraph triplets extracted from radiology reports and retrieved relevant subgraphs to condition explanation generation alongside visual features in a multimodal transformer architecture. Although KG-LLaVA significantly improved factual grounding and explanation relevance compared to standard VLMs, it relied exclusively on single-domain radiology knowledge and did not incorporate broader clinical reasoning involving disease mechanisms or treatment context.

# Background and Related Work
### 1. Jain et al. (2021) – RadGraph: Extracting Radiology Knowledge Graphs
In the work by Jain et al. [2021], the authors introduced RadGraph, a large-scale dataset and structured information extraction framework for constructing knowledge graphs from radiology reports. They developed a joint entity and relation extraction model based on DyGIE++ to identify clinical observations, anatomical locations, and medical conditions along with their semantic relationships from free-text reports. Although RadGraph enabled high-quality structured representation of radiological findings, it was limited to imaging-specific knowledge and excluded broader clinical context such as symptoms, disease mechanisms, and treatment information.

### 2. Li et al. (2022) – Clinical-Longformer and Clinical-BigBird for Long Medical Texts
In the study by Li et al. [2022], the authors proposed Clinical-Longformer and Clinical-BigBird, transformer architectures designed to process long clinical documents exceeding standard token limits. They utilized sparse attention mechanisms and pre-trained the models on millions of clinical notes from MIMIC-III, evaluating performance across named entity recognition, question answering, and document classification tasks. While the models significantly improved understanding of long clinical narratives, they focused on isolated document processing rather than integrating structured knowledge across multiple clinical domains.

### 3. Wu et al. (2024) – Medical Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation
In the work by Wu et al. [2024], the authors introduced MedGraphRAG, a graph-based retrieval-augmented generation framework aimed at improving factual reliability and safety in medical large language models. They constructed a hierarchical medical graph linking user documents, biomedical literature, and UMLS concepts, employing structured retrieval strategies to provide evidence-grounded responses. Although the framework achieved strong performance on medical question-answering benchmarks, it focused on text-based reasoning and did not integrate multimodal clinical image understanding.

### 4. Feng et al. (2025) – Knowledge Graph-Based Thought for Biomedical Reasoning
In the study by Feng et al. [2025], the authors proposed the Knowledge Graph-Based Thought (KGT) framework to reduce hallucinations in biomedical question answering systems. Their approach involved extracting key entities from questions, inferring relational paths within a biomedical knowledge graph, constructing relevant subgraphs, and guiding large language models using structured graph reasoning. While KGT demonstrated improved factual consistency in cancer-related question answering, its evaluation remained restricted to a single biomedical domain and purely textual inputs.

### 5. Li et al. (2025) – Graph-Enhanced Vision–Language Models for Explainable Medical Imaging
In the work by Chenjun Li et al. [2025], the authors addressed explainable medical image analysis by integrating graph-based medical knowledge with vision–language models for diabetic retinopathy staging. They constructed biologically informed graphs from imaging features, applied graph neural networks for classification, and transformed graph-level explanations into textual descriptions to instruction-tune a VLM capable of producing diagnostic explanations. Although the method improved interpretability and diagnostic accuracy, it relied heavily on domain-specific graph construction and did not incorporate multi-domain clinical knowledge.

### 6. Abdullah and Kim (2025) – Multimodal Radiology Knowledge Graph Generation
In the study by Abdullah and Kim [2025], the authors introduced VLM-KG, a framework for generating multimodal radiology knowledge graphs by jointly processing medical images and radiology reports. They leveraged vision–language models to extract clinical entities and relationships from both visual and textual modalities, producing structured knowledge graphs that captured richer radiological semantics than text-only approaches.

While the method enhanced radiology-specific knowledge representation, it remained limited to imaging domains and did not integrate disease mechanisms or treatment knowledge.
